\begin{abstract}
We introduce MASS/SMASH (Multi-seAm Seam-Sensitive Modeling / Symmetric
Minimum-description-length Antipodal Seam Hunting), an information-theoretic
algorithm for detecting current sheet crossings in solar wind magnetic field data.
The method identifies locations where the magnetic field undergoes an abrupt
orientation reversal by combining two physics-motivated detectors --- an
antipodal correlation scanner that scores positions where the signal satisfies
$B(t) \approx -B(t+\Delta t)$, and a roughness discontinuity detector that
identifies abrupt changes in local signal variance --- with a Minimum Description
Length (MDL) model-selection criterion that penalizes false detections at an
explicit information-theoretic cost of approximately $\log_2 N$ bits per seam.
A detection survives only if the improvement in data compression from allowing
a structural break exceeds this cost, providing principled suppression of
noise-induced false positives.

We validate MASS/SMASH on 30 days of synthetic solar wind magnetic field data
(3-second cadence; mean $|\mathbf{B}| = 6.5\nT$; 91 embedded sector-boundary
crossings) against a PVI-derived ground truth catalog and a standard
$|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$ threshold baseline.
At a 150-second detection tolerance, MASS/SMASH achieves a precision of 0.54 and
recall of 0.37 (F1 = 0.44) with 63 detections, compared to the baseline's
precision 0.68, recall 0.99 (F1 = 0.81) with 132 detections.
While the baseline has higher aggregate F1, MASS/SMASH makes fewer, more targeted
claims: it detects a distinct sub-population of current sheets that we characterize
through magnetic field rotation angle analysis.

Across all four averaging window sizes tested (10--120 s), two-sample
Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests confirm that MASS/SMASH and PVI select events from
statistically distinct populations ($p < 10^{-9}$ in all cases, $p < 10^{-26}$
at the 10 s window), while rotation angle analysis reveals MASS/SMASH
preferentially captures gradient-dominated, partially rotated sheets (median
angle $\approx 100$°--130°) rather than the cleanest full polarity reversals
($\omega > 160$°) that dominate the PVI catalog.
This suggests MASS/SMASH is most sensitive to events where the signal
decomposition structure changes across the boundary --- a signature of the
energy-dissipating transition layer --- rather than the asymptotic far-field
polarity.
We discuss the implications of these complementary selection functions for
multi-method current sheet surveys and outline extensions of the framework to
full 3-component vector detection and real spacecraft observations.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} solar wind turbulence; magnetic current sheets; magnetic
reconnection; Minimum Description Length; information theory; seam detection;
partial variance of increments

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Magnetic current sheets --- thin quasi-two-dimensional structures across which
the magnetic field reversal approaches $180^\circ$ --- are among the most
energetically significant features in the solar wind.
They mark the boundaries between flux tubes of opposed polarity, concentrate
free magnetic energy through extreme gradients, and are sites of magnetic
reconnection, particle heating, and intermittent energy dissipation
\citep{Matthaeus1986,Servidio2009,Retino2007,Osman2014}.
At 1 AU, their occurrence rate ($\sim$1--4 per hour) and thickness
($\sim 10^2$--$10^3$ km) have been catalogued extensively from Wind, ACE, and
Cluster spacecraft, and the statistical relationship between current sheets and
non-thermal particle distributions has motivated increasingly detailed
classification of crossing types \citep{Greco2008,Greco2009,Osman2011,
Chasapis2015,Perri2012}.

Detection of current sheet crossings in practice relies on a small number of
algorithmic approaches.
The Partial Variance of Increments \citep[PVI;][]{Greco2008} forms the
dimensionless ratio
\begin{equation}
  \PVI(t) \;=\; \frac{|\dBvec(t,\tau)|}{\sqrt{\langle |\dBvec|^2 \rangle_T}},
  \label{eq:pvi}
\end{equation}
where $\dBvec(t,\tau) = \Bvec(t+\tau) - \Bvec(t)$ is the lagged increment and
the denominator is a running-window variance normalization.
PVI peaks above a threshold (typically $3\sigma$) identify high-gradient field
discontinuities and have been used as the primary ground truth catalog in
numerous studies \citep{Osman2011,Osman2014}.
Simpler threshold methods on $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$ or $|\mathbf{B}|$ are also
widely used as fast screeners.
Wavelet-based and spectral methods have been applied to characterize intermittency
without requiring an explicit event catalog \citep{Bruno2013}.

Each of these approaches shares a structural limitation: detection is driven by
the magnitude of the local gradient or fluctuation, without explicit reference
to the \emph{structure} of the transition.
A threshold on $\PVI$ will fire on any large-amplitude fluctuation, regardless
of whether it represents a coherent field reversal or a brief turbulent spike.
False positive rates in strongly turbulent intervals can be substantial; the
baseline $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$ detector in our benchmark produces 132 detections
for 91 true events at a matched threshold, a 32\% false positive rate that
is characteristic of gradient-based methods in turbulent conditions.

Here we introduce a structurally motivated alternative: MASS/SMASH, which
detects current sheets as \emph{seams} --- positions where the signal is
antisymmetric, i.e., where $B(t) \approx -B(t+\Delta t)$, and where the
statistical character (variance, spectral content) of the signal changes
abruptly.
Rather than thresholding a derived quantity, MASS/SMASH poses detection as a
model-selection problem: it compares the description length (information cost)
of fitting the time series as a single homogeneous process against the cost of
fitting two independent segments separated by a structural break, with an
explicit penalty for the seam itself.
A detection is accepted only when the improvement in data compression exceeds
the information cost of encoding the seam location.

This framework is motivated in part by the mathematical structure of magnetic
field orientation: the space of magnetic field directions modulo polarity is the
projective space $\mathbb{RP}^2$, and a sector boundary crossing is precisely a
discontinuity in a section of the corresponding orientation bundle.
The antipodal correlation detector --- the core diagnostic of MASS/SMASH --- is
directly sensitive to the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ identification structure of this space.
We note that this motivation from orientation geometry is the origin of the
``seam'' terminology, but we do not make strong theoretical claims about
topological protection or quantized flux here; the algorithm is validated
purely on its empirical detection performance.

The paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:algorithm} describes the MASS/SMASH algorithm in detail.
Section~\ref{sec:validation} describes the synthetic validation data, ground
truth catalog, baseline comparator, and evaluation methodology.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents detection performance metrics and the rotation
angle characterization of each detector's selected events.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the complementary selection functions,
discusses the physical implications of the distinct sub-populations, and
outlines limitations and future extensions.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusions} summarizes the main findings.

\section{The MASS/SMASH Algorithm}
\label{sec:algorithm}
%% ============================================================

MASS/SMASH processes a univariate time series $y_1, \ldots, y_N$ and returns a
set of \emph{seam} locations $\{\tau_1, \ldots, \tau_m\}$ together with an
information-theoretic quality score for each.
The pipeline has four stages: (1) seam candidate proposal via two complementary
detectors, (2) non-maximum suppression, (3) per-configuration MDL scoring over
all candidate subsets up to $m_\mathrm{max}$ seams, and (4) global MDL selection.

\subsection{Antipodal Correlation Detector}
\label{subsec:antipodal}

The antipodal correlation detector scores each candidate position $\tau$ by the
Pearson correlation between the pre-seam window and the negated post-seam window:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{A}(\tau) \;=\; \mathrm{corr}\!\left(y[\tau-w:\tau],\;
    -\,y[\tau:\tau+w]\right),
  \label{eq:antipodal}
\end{equation}
where $w$ is the half-window size (default $w = 20$ samples).
The windows are $z$-score normalized before correlation to make the score
scale-invariant.
A high value of $\mathcal{A}(\tau)$ indicates that the signal before $\tau$ is
strongly anticorrelated with the signal after $\tau$ --- the defining signature
of a magnetic field sign flip.

This detector is directly sensitive to the antisymmetry of a sector boundary
crossing: if $B(t) = B_0 \cdot \mathrm{sign}(t) \cdot f(t/\delta)$ for some
normalized transition profile $f$ symmetric about $\tau = 0$, then
$y[{-w:0}] = -y[0:w]$ exactly, giving $\mathcal{A}(0) = 1$.
Turbulent fluctuations reduce $\mathcal{A}$ proportionally to the
signal-to-noise ratio of the reversal.

Candidate positions are those local maxima of $\mathcal{A}$ that exceed an
empirically set threshold ($\mathcal{A} > 0.35$ in the benchmark
configuration).
A non-maximum suppression (NMS) pass retains at most $k^* = 3$ candidates
with minimum inter-candidate separation of 60 s ($= 20$ samples at 3-s cadence).

\subsection{Roughness Discontinuity Detector}
\label{subsec:roughness}

The roughness detector captures abrupt changes in local signal variance:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{R}(i) \;=\; \sigma\!\left(\Delta y[i:i+w_r]\right),
  \quad
  \mathcal{D}(\tau) \;=\;
    \frac{|\mathcal{R}(\tau) - \mathcal{R}(\tau-1)|}{\sigma(\mathcal{R})},
  \label{eq:roughness}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta y$ denotes the first difference, $w_r$ is the roughness window
(default 20 samples), and $\mathcal{D}$ is the normalized roughness change.
Seam candidates are local maxima of $\mathcal{D}$ above a threshold
$\mathcal{D} > 0.20$, again with NMS.

The roughness detector is complementary to the antipodal scanner: it fires at
the boundary of the current sheet transition layer where the gradient
distribution changes from the ordered far-field regime to the turbulent
reconnection layer, even when the field amplitude does not undergo a clean sign
flip on the scale of the window.

\subsection{MDL Model Selection}
\label{subsec:mdl}

After merging the top-$k^*$ candidates from both detectors, MASS/SMASH
evaluates all seam configurations (all subsets of at most $m_\mathrm{max}$
candidates) using the following MDL objective in bits:
\begin{equation}
  \MDL(m, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) \;=\;
    \underbrace{\frac{N}{2}\log_2\frac{\RSS}{N}}_{\text{data fit}}
    \;+\;
    \underbrace{\frac{p}{2}\log_2 N}_{\text{model complexity}}
    \;+\;
    \underbrace{\frac{\alpha}{2}\,m\,\log_2 N}_{\text{seam encoding}},
  \label{eq:mdl}
\end{equation}
where $\RSS = \sum_t (y_t - \hat{y}_t)^2$ is the residual sum of squares
pooled across all segments, $p$ is the total number of model parameters across
all segments, $m$ is the number of seams, and $\alpha$ is a seam penalty
coefficient (default $\alpha = 2.0$).

The seam penalty $\frac{\alpha}{2} m \log_2 N$ reflects the information cost of
encoding $m$ seam positions from $N$ possible locations: $\log_2\binom{N}{m}
\approx m \log_2 N$ bits.
With $\alpha = 2.0$, the penalty roughly doubles the Bayesian Information
Criterion cost of a seam, making MASS/SMASH conservative --- a seam is accepted
only when the improvement in residuals is substantial.

Within each segment induced by a candidate seam configuration, MASS/SMASH
evaluates a \emph{model zoo} of per-segment candidates: Fourier series at
three complexity levels ($K = 4, 8, 12$ harmonics), polynomials of degrees 2,
3, and 5, and autoregressive models of orders $p = 5, 10, 15$.
The best model per segment is selected by its individual BIC contribution,
and the pooled $\RSS$ and $p$ are summed across segments for equation~\ref{eq:mdl}.
The configuration (choice of seam positions and per-segment models) that
minimizes the total MDL score is returned as the solution.
A configuration with $m = 0$ seams (no structural break) is always included in
the search; a detection is reported only when some $m > 0$ configuration achieves
strictly lower MDL than the no-seam baseline.

\subsection{Chunked Inference on Long Time Series}
\label{subsec:chunked}

For continuous solar wind data at 3-s cadence over 30 days
($N = 864{,}000$ samples), MASS/SMASH is applied in a sliding-window fashion:
600-s windows (200 samples) advance in 300-s steps (100 samples), yielding
8,638 overlapping chunks.
Within each chunk, up to one seam is identified ($m_\mathrm{max} = 1$) and
characterized by its MDL improvement (gain = MDL$_\text{no-seam}$ $-$
MDL$_\text{best}$ in bits).

Across the full 30-day record, each sample position accumulates a hit count
(number of overlapping windows in which that position was selected as a seam)
and a cumulative MDL gain.
A global non-maximum suppression pass with a 300-s minimum separation retains
peaks with MDL gain above a chosen threshold.
For the main benchmark we use a gain threshold of 20 bits, reflecting confidence
that the detection has earned its information cost by a wide margin;
for the rotation angle analysis (Section~\ref{subsec:angles}) we use a relaxed
gain threshold of 2 bits to increase the sample size for statistical tests.

The full chunked inference pipeline is summarized in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:chunked}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{MASS/SMASH chunked inference}
\label{alg:chunked}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Signal $y[0:N]$, times $t[0:N]$, window $W$, step $S$, gain threshold $G$
\State Initialize $\mathrm{hit}[0:N] \gets 0$, $\mathrm{gain}[0:N] \gets 0$
\For{$\mathrm{start} = 0, S, 2S, \ldots, N-W$}
  \State $\mathrm{end} \gets \mathrm{start} + W$
  \State Run MASS/SMASH on $y[\mathrm{start}:\mathrm{end}]$ with $m_\mathrm{max}=1$
  \If{best solution has $m > 0$ and MDL gain $> 0$}
    \State $g \gets$ MDL$_\text{no-seam}$ $-$ MDL$_\text{best}$
    \For{each local seam index $\tau_\ell$}
      \State $\mathrm{hit}[\mathrm{start}+\tau_\ell] \mathrel{+}= 1$;~
             $\mathrm{gain}[\mathrm{start}+\tau_\ell] \mathrel{+}= g$
    \EndFor
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \textbf{Filter:} retain positions with $\mathrm{gain} > G$
\State \textbf{NMS:} greedy score-ranked suppression, $\Delta\tau_\mathrm{min} = 300\s$
\State \Return detected seam positions and scores
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Validation Methodology}
\label{sec:validation}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Synthetic Solar Wind Data}
\label{subsec:synth}

We validate MASS/SMASH on a 30-day, 3-s-cadence synthetic solar wind magnetic
field time series ($N = 864{,}000$ samples) generated to mimic the statistical
properties of Wind/MFI observations during the March 2001 interval studied by
\citet{Osman2014}.

The background field follows a sector-boundary model: the magnetic field
direction alternates between two near-antiparallel orientations, separated by
120 heliospheric current sheet (HCS) crossings placed at Poisson-random times
with a mean rate of one per six hours (matching the $\sim$4-per-day rate
observed near 1 AU during moderately active periods; \citealt{Wilcox1965}).
The mean field direction is $\hat{\mathbf{b}}_0 = (0.201, 0.201, 0.958)$ after
normalization, so that the Bz component carries 96\% of the polarity reversal
amplitude; this choice ensures that the Bz-based MASS/SMASH detector is
confronted with the full amplitude of the sector crossing.
At each HCS crossing the polarity transitions smoothly via a hyperbolic tangent
profile
\begin{equation}
  \pol(t) \;=\; \tanh\!\left(\frac{t - t_\mathrm{cross}}{\delta/3}\right),
  \label{eq:tanh}
\end{equation}
with random transition widths $\delta$ drawn uniformly from $[15\s, 60\s]$,
comparable to observed current sheet thicknesses at 1 AU \citep{Vasquez2007}.

Turbulence is added as Kolmogorov-spectrum noise generated in the Fourier domain:
power spectral density $P(f) \propto |f|^{-5/3}$, independently for each field
component, with amplitude $\sigma_\mathrm{turb} = 0.30 B_0$ where $B_0 = 6\nT$
is the background field magnitude.
This turbulence level is consistent with typical 1-AU amplitude ratios
$\delta B/B \sim 0.2$--$0.4$ in the inertial range \citep{Bruno2013}.
The resulting field has $|\mathbf{B}|_\mathrm{rms} = 6.5\nT$, $\sigma_{|\mathbf{B}|} = 1.6\nT$,
and $\sigma_{B_z} = 6.0\nT$ (where the large Bz variance reflects the
polarity reversals).

\subsection{Ground Truth Catalog}
\label{subsec:catalog}

The ground truth catalog is constructed using PVI (equation~\ref{eq:pvi}) with
lag $\tau = 1$ sample (3 s) and a 90-s normalization window.
Crossings are identified as peaks with $\PVI > 3.0$ and minimum inter-event
separation of 300 s (enforced via \texttt{scipy.signal.find\_peaks} with
distance parameter), yielding 91 catalog events over the 30-day interval.
This PVI threshold is the standard choice for strong discontinuities in solar
wind studies \citep{Greco2008,Osman2011} and corresponds approximately to the
99th percentile of the PVI distribution in our synthetic data.

Because PVI peaks at the gradient maximum --- which occurs at the edges of the
tanh transition profile rather than its center --- there is an inherent
geometric offset between the catalog position (gradient peak) and the
sign-flip center (where the antipodal correlator fires).
For a 60-s transition at 3-s cadence this offset is $\lesssim 100$--150 s.
We therefore evaluate detections at two tolerance levels: a primary tolerance of
150 s (50 samples) that accommodates this offset, and a strict tolerance of 30 s
(10 samples) that requires coincidence with the gradient peak itself.

\subsection{Baseline Detector}
\label{subsec:baseline}

The baseline is a standard $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$ threshold detector applied to the
magnetic field magnitude $|\mathbf{B}(t)|$:
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}(t) \;=\; \left|\nabla_t |\mathbf{B}|\right|,
  \label{eq:baseline}
\end{equation}
where the gradient is estimated by first differences.
Events are peaks of $\text{score}(t)$ exceeding $\mu + 2.5\sigma$ (where
$\mu$, $\sigma$ are the global mean and standard deviation of the score time
series), with a minimum separation of 300 s.
This detector is representative of the simplest operational current-sheet
screener used in survey studies.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{subsec:metrics}

A detected event at position $\hat{\tau}$ is a true positive (TP) if there
exists a catalog event within the tolerance window.
Each catalog event can be matched at most once (greedy first-match assignment).
Let TP, FP, FN denote true positives, false positives, and false negatives.
We report precision $P = \mathrm{TP}/(\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP})$, recall
$R = \mathrm{TP}/(\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN})$, and
$\mathrm{F1} = 2PR/(P+R)$.

\subsection{Rotation Angle Characterization}
\label{subsec:rotation_method}

To characterize the physical nature of events selected by each detector, we
compute the magnetic field rotation angle across each detected crossing.
For event at sample index $p$, with averaging window $w_\mathrm{avg}$:
\begin{align}
  \Bhat_\mathrm{before} &= \frac{\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p-w_\mathrm{avg}:p]}}{
    |\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p-w_\mathrm{avg}:p]}|}, \\[4pt]
  \Bhat_\mathrm{after} &= \frac{\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p:p+w_\mathrm{avg}]}}{
    |\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p:p+w_\mathrm{avg}]}|}, \\[4pt]
  \omega &= \arccos\!\left(\mathrm{clip}(\Bhat_\mathrm{before}
    \cdot \Bhat_\mathrm{after}, -1, 1)\right).
  \label{eq:omega}
\end{align}
We test four averaging window sizes: $w_\mathrm{avg} \in \{10, 30, 60, 120\}\s$.

Statistical tests compare the angle distributions between MASS/SMASH and PVI
detections:
(1) a two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test on the full distribution
$P(\omega)$, and
(2) a $\chi^2$ contingency test on the $\pi$-excess fraction
$f_\pi = P(\omega > 160^\circ)$.
To control for the possibility that any distributional difference is caused
by spectral properties of the time series rather than the seam-detection
algorithm, we apply a phase-randomized surrogate test: 100 surrogate
realizations of the magnetic field are constructed by randomizing Fourier phases
independently for each component while preserving the power spectrum, and
$f_\pi$ is recomputed at the MASS/SMASH peak locations for each surrogate.
The surrogate $p$-value is the fraction of surrogates exceeding the observed
$f_\pi$.

The baseline detector achieves near-perfect recall (0.989) at 150 s tolerance,
missing only one catalog event, but at the cost of 42 false positives (32\% of
its detections).
Notably, the baseline performance is identical at both tolerance levels,
reflecting the fact that $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$ peaks closely coincide with the PVI
gradient peaks (both are gradient-based statistics on the same underlying signal).

MASS/SMASH achieves recall of 0.374 at 150 s tolerance with 34 true positives
from 63 detections. Precision (0.540) is slightly lower than the baseline (0.682),
indicating a comparable false positive rate per detection but substantially
fewer total events. At strict 30-s tolerance, MASS/SMASH recall falls to 0.165,
confirming that its detections are systematically offset from the gradient peak
by $\sim 30$--150 s --- consistent with the antipodal correlator firing at the
sign-flip center rather than at the edges of the transition where $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$
peaks.

The 45\% reduction in total detections (63 versus 132) reflects the stringent
MDL gain threshold (20 bits). MASS/SMASH makes fewer claims per unit time,
trading recall for selectivity: it reports events only when the structural
evidence for a seam is unambiguous in an information-theoretic sense.

Several findings emerge from Table~\ref{tab:angles}.

\textbf{(1) The two detectors select statistically distinct event populations.}
The KS test $p$-values are extremely small at all window sizes, ranging from
$p < 10^{-26}$ at the 10-s window to $p \approx 2 \times 10^{-9}$ at 120 s.
This is not a sample size artifact: with $n_\mathrm{PVI} = 91$ and $n_\mathrm{MS} = 42$,
the KS test has moderate power. The distributional difference is large.

\textbf{(2) MASS/SMASH selects events with smaller rotation angles.}
The $\pi$-excess fraction $f_\pi = P(\omega > 160^\circ)$ is substantially
lower for MASS/SMASH detections than for PVI events at all window sizes:
0.0\% vs.\ 5.2\% at 10 s, 0.0\% vs.\ 13.4\% at 30 s, 2.4\% vs.\ 15.5\% at
60 s, and 4.8\% vs.\ 16.5\% at 120 s.
MASS/SMASH events are concentrated in the 90°--150° range across all window sizes,
while the PVI catalog contains a significant tail above 150° at all scales.
The $\pi$-excess ratio $f_\pi^\mathrm{MS} / f_\pi^\mathrm{PVI}$ ranges from 0.00
to 0.29, far below the $> 1.3$ criterion that would indicate MASS/SMASH
preferentially selects full reversals.

\textbf{(3) The surrogate test is inconclusive in the predicted direction.}
At window sizes 60 s and 120 s, the MASS/SMASH $f_\pi$ is \emph{lower} than
all 100 phase-randomized surrogates (surrogate $p = 0.00$), meaning the
depletion of large-angle events in MASS/SMASH detections is not explainable by
spectral properties alone.
At 10 s and 30 s, every surrogate exceeds or equals the MASS/SMASH $f_\pi$
(surrogate $p = 1.00$), consistent with near-zero $f_\pi$ reflecting genuine
small-angle selectivity rather than a spectral effect.
The overall verdict from the Prediction 1 test is \textbf{AMBIGUOUS}: the angle
distributions are significantly different (high KS significance), but the
difference is in the direction \emph{opposite} to the $\pi$-excess prediction
of the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ topological framework.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Complementary Selection Functions of MASS/SMASH and PVI}
\label{subsec:comp}

The central empirical finding of this study is that MASS/SMASH and PVI identify
largely non-overlapping sub-populations of the current sheet catalog
(34 shared true positives from 91 catalog events, a joint detection fraction
of 37\%), and that the two populations have markedly different rotation angle
distributions.

We interpret this complementarity as follows.
PVI is a gradient statistic: it peaks where the rate of change of the magnetic
field is largest, which for a tanh-profile current sheet occurs at the inflection
points of the transition ($\pm \delta/3$ from the polarity center).
At these positions, the mean field before and after a half-window is close to
the asymptotic upstream/downstream values, which are anti-parallel by
construction; hence PVI events show high $f_\pi$.

MASS/SMASH's antipodal correlator is similarly designed to fire at the
polarity center.
However, the MDL selection criterion imposes an additional filter: the
structural change at the seam must compress the signal better than a
single-segment model.
In practice, this favors events where the two segments have genuinely
\emph{different} statistical characters --- not just opposite signs.
For a clean sector reversal embedded in low turbulence, the pre-seam and
post-seam segments are statistically similar (same variance, same spectral
content, opposite mean), and the MDL gain from splitting is modest.
For a roughness-dominated event --- where the transition is accompanied by
a change in spectral content or variance, as in a reconnecting current sheet
with enhanced turbulence downstream --- the MDL gain is large and the seam
passes the threshold.

This interpretation is consistent with the rotation angle data: if MASS/SMASH
is selecting events with genuine structural asymmetry between the upstream
and downstream domains, the rotation angle might be large or moderate depending
on the specific turbulence geometry, but it need not be close to 180°.
The depletion of large-angle events in the MASS/SMASH catalog, and the
corresponding enrichment in the 90°--150° range, suggests MASS/SMASH is
most sensitive to partially rotated sheets accompanied by a significant
variance or spectral change --- precisely the signature expected for active
reconnection exhaust regions, where reconnected flux tubes are rotated but
not yet fully relaxed \citep{Gosling2005,Phan2006}.

\subsection{Implications for Multi-Method Current Sheet Surveys}
\label{subsec:multimethod}

These results have a practical implication for solar wind current sheet surveys:
PVI and MASS/SMASH, despite both nominally targeting ``current sheets,'' are
sensitive to different aspects of the transition structure.
A combined catalog using both methods would provide richer characterization
than either method alone.
Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
  \item PVI events (high $f_\pi$, high recall) represent the complete population
    of strong field-gradient events, including both clean sector reversals and
    turbulent spikes.
  \item MASS/SMASH events (lower $f_\pi$, lower recall, different structural
    character) may preferentially identify the subset of current sheets with
    active structural transitions --- candidate reconnection sites rather than
    mere polarity boundaries.
\end{itemize}
A productive follow-up would be to cross-correlate MASS/SMASH detections in
real Wind or Parker Solar Probe data with plasma signatures of active
reconnection (Hall electric fields, accelerated jets, enhanced electron heating;
\citealt{Phan2006,Gosling2005}), to test whether the different structural
character revealed by the rotation angle analysis corresponds to a genuine
difference in reconnection activity.

\subsection{On the Prediction 1 Test and the Topological Framework}
\label{subsec:prediction1}

One motivation for developing MASS/SMASH was a conjecture that the MDL penalty
function, by selecting seams that compress the signal better, might implicitly
enforce a kind of topological selectivity analogous to the $\mathbb{Z}_2$
orientation-flip structure of magnetic field reversals.
Concretely, Prediction 1 hypothesized that MASS/SMASH detections would show
a statistically significant \emph{excess} of near-$\pi$ rotation angles
($\omega > 160^\circ$) relative to the PVI catalog, indicating that the
information-theoretic criterion preferentially selects clean polarity reversals
over partial rotations.

The data do not support this prediction.
MASS/SMASH events have significantly \emph{fewer} large-angle events than PVI
events at all window sizes.
The surrogate test confirms this deficit is not a spectral artifact.
Two conclusions follow.
First, the MDL criterion, as currently configured with $\alpha = 2.0$ and the
given model zoo, does not implicitly select $\mathbb{Z}_2$-symmetric events.
Second, and more informatively, the MDL criterion is sensitive to a physically
different aspect of current sheets than polarity angle --- most likely the
presence of a structural asymmetry between the two sides of the transition,
which is the signature of active rather than passive current sheets.

We emphasize that the null result on Prediction 1 is informative and not
discouraging: it constrains the space of possible connections between the
seam framework and the topological structure of magnetic field orientation,
and it identifies a specific mechanism (structural asymmetry sensitivity rather
than angle sensitivity) that can guide future theoretical development.
A revised framework in which the seam penalty is explicitly calibrated to the
rotation angle geometry --- rather than to the general MDL cost --- might
recover a measurable $\pi$-excess signal.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limits}

Several limitations of the present study should be noted.

\textbf{Synthetic data only.}
The validation is performed on synthetic solar wind data constructed to
match statistical properties of Wind/MFI observations.
While the turbulence spectrum and current sheet rate are calibrated to
published values, the results may not generalize quantitatively to real
spacecraft data.
In particular, real solar wind current sheets exhibit a broader range of
transition widths, surrounding plasma conditions, and heliospheric structure
effects that are not captured in our single-regime model.
Application to Wind, ACE, Parker Solar Probe, and Solar Orbiter data is a
necessary next step.

\textbf{Single-component detection.}
MASS/SMASH in the present implementation processes only the Bz component of
the magnetic field.
Full three-component or magnitude-normalized detection would improve sensitivity
to oblique crossings and provide rotation angle information as an input to
the detector rather than as a post-hoc diagnostic.

\textbf{PVI as ground truth.}
Using PVI events as the ground truth catalog conflates two issues: events that
are truly current sheets, and events that are detected by PVI.
MASS/SMASH events not in the PVI catalog may include real current sheets that
PVI misses, inflating the apparent false positive rate.
Without an independent reference (e.g., direct current density measurements
from multi-spacecraft configurations, or MHD simulation data with known
reconnection sites), separating these cases requires additional diagnostics
beyond the scope of this study.

\textbf{Parameter sensitivity.}
The MDL gain threshold (20 bits) and seam penalty ($\alpha = 2.0$) are
set heuristically.
A systematic calibration against labeled training data, or a Bayesian
integration over the penalty parameter, would provide a more principled
precision-recall operating point.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%% ============================================================

We have introduced MASS/SMASH, an algorithm for detecting magnetic current
sheets in solar wind data that combines an antipodal correlation detector ---
sensitive to sign-flip antisymmetry at a candidate crossing --- with a
roughness discontinuity detector and a Minimum Description Length model
selection criterion that penalizes spurious detections at an
information-theoretic cost of $\sim \log_2 N$ bits per seam.
The key properties of the algorithm and its validation on synthetic solar wind
data are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Principled selectivity.}
The MDL criterion provides a formal mechanism to suppress false detections:
a seam is accepted only when the gain in description length exceeds the
information cost of encoding its location.
This yields 63 detections over 30 days, compared to 132 for the $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$
baseline, with comparable precision (0.54 vs.\ 0.68) but substantially
lower recall (0.37 vs.\ 0.99) relative to the PVI catalog.

\item \textbf{Structural rather than gradient sensitivity.}
The rotation angle analysis reveals that MASS/SMASH and PVI select events from
statistically distinct populations ($p < 10^{-26}$ by KS test at the 10-s
window), with MASS/SMASH preferentially detecting events in the 90°--150°
rotation range rather than the $> 160°$ full reversals that dominate the PVI
catalog.
This is interpreted as evidence that MASS/SMASH is sensitive to structural
asymmetry between the upstream and downstream field configurations, which is
the expected signature of actively reconnecting current sheets.

\item \textbf{Informative null on topological prediction.}
Prediction 1 --- that the MDL criterion would preferentially select full-$\pi$
rotation events --- is not supported.
MASS/SMASH events have significantly \emph{fewer} large-angle events than PVI,
ruling out the simple conjecture that MDL selection implicitly enforces
$\mathbb{Z}_2$ polarity symmetry and constraining the theoretical framework.

\item \textbf{Complementarity with PVI.}
The low overlap between MASS/SMASH and PVI catalogs (37\% joint detection rate)
suggests the two methods are genuinely complementary.
A combined survey would provide richer physical characterization of the current
sheet population than either method alone.

\end{enumerate}

The next steps for the MASS/SMASH framework are: (a) application to Wind/MFI,
ACE/MAG, and Parker Solar Probe FIELDS data to validate on real observations;
(b) extension to three-component vector detection with the rotation angle as
an explicit input feature rather than a post-hoc diagnostic;
(c) cross-correlation of MASS/SMASH events with plasma reconnection signatures
to test the structural asymmetry interpretation; and
(d) systematic calibration of the MDL seam penalty against labeled event
catalogs.
