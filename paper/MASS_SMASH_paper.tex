\begin{abstract}
Standard threshold detectors for polarity-reversal events suffer from
\emph{precision collapse}: as broadband noise increases, false detections
accumulate faster than the threshold adapts, rendering catalogs unreliable
at high turbulence.
We introduce MASS/SMASH, a conservative change-point detector that addresses
this by combining an \emph{antipodal correlation pre-filter} --- which scores
candidate positions for sign-reversal antisymmetry rather than raw gradient
magnitude --- with a Minimum Description Length (MDL) model-selection step
whose acceptance threshold is denominated in bits of compression gain rather
than multiples of a signal-derived statistic.

We validate MASS/SMASH as a complementary selector for solar wind magnetic
current sheet detection across three synthetic benchmarks.
In a single-regime benchmark (30 days, 120 injected crossings, 30\%
Kolmogorov turbulence), MASS/SMASH achieves precision 0.921 / recall 0.483,
contributing 24 true events missed by the standard PVI detector while sharing
a 95\% joint catalog at $\sim$5\% combined false discovery fraction.
An SNR sweep across turbulence amplitudes 5\%--50\% of the background field
shows MASS/SMASH precision at 1.000 at every level tested, while a
$d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$ threshold baseline falls to precision 0.037 at 50\%
turbulence (758 spurious detections for 28 true events); PVI holds at
0.944--0.947 via its local variance normalization.
A contaminated benchmark with 40 non-antipodal confounding events shows the
baseline fires on 98\% of confounders, MASS/SMASH on 18\%, and PVI on 8\%,
with MASS/SMASH and PVI exhibiting complementary confounder-rejection
mechanisms.

All quantitative results are from synthetic data; application to real
spacecraft observations is the primary direction for future work.
We discuss the precision-recall trade-off, a $\pm 30\s$ detection localization
artifact, and extensions to magnetospheric physics, EEG, climate, and power
systems.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} change-point detection; sign-flip; antipodal correlation;
Minimum Description Length; precision stability; solar wind; current sheets;
partial variance of increments; structural break; information theory

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{The Precision-Stability Problem in Sign-Flip Detection}

Many physical and engineered systems produce time series that undergo
\emph{sign-flip change-points}: transitions from a positive steady state near
$+\mu_0$ to an equal-magnitude negative state near $-\mu_0$, embedded in
broadband noise.
In the solar wind, magnetic field polarity reversals at sector boundaries and
current sheet crossings exhibit this structure in the radial ($B_r$) or
out-of-ecliptic ($B_z$) components.
In EEG, reference electrode inversions and certain seizure complexes produce
sign flips in individual channel signals.
Climate indices (Niño-3.4, PDO, AMO) undergo polarity reversals between
warm and cold phases embedded in interannual variability.
In AC power systems, line-to-line faults and inverter failures produce
characteristic sign-flip patterns in current waveforms.

A practical challenge shared across these domains is \emph{precision collapse}:
standard threshold detectors fire on any large local gradient, and as noise
amplitude increases, the number of spurious detections grows faster than the
threshold can adapt.
A detector that fires at $\mu + k\sigma$ of the gradient distribution will
identify every true event (achieving perfect recall), but the false discovery
fraction increases monotonically with turbulence because the gradient
distribution develops heavier tails.
Figure~\ref{fig:snr_sweep} (Section~\ref{subsec:snr}) documents this
quantitatively in our solar wind benchmark: the threshold baseline achieves
precision 1.000 at 5\% turbulence but falls to 0.037 at 50\% turbulence (758
detections for 28 true events).

For applications where the downstream analysis is expensive
--- reconnection jet identification, seizure classification, fault diagnosis
--- a catalog with even 50\% false discovery fraction is unusable.
What is needed is a method whose precision is stable (or gracefully degrading)
as noise increases, even at the cost of lower recall.

\subsection{MASS/SMASH: A Conservative Complementary Selector}

We introduce MASS/SMASH (Multi-seAm Seam-Sensitive Modeling / Symmetric
Minimum-description-length Antipodal Seam Hunting).
Its design goal is not to maximize recall but to provide a conservative,
structurally selective complement to gradient-based detectors like PVI:
a method that contributes non-redundant true detections while maintaining
low false discovery fraction across a wide range of noise conditions.

Two design choices support this goal.

\textbf{Structural pre-filtering via antipodal correlation.}
Before evaluating any MDL criterion, MASS/SMASH scores each candidate position
$\tau$ for antisymmetry between the pre- and post-window signals:
$\mathcal{A}(\tau) = \mathrm{corr}(y[\tau-w:\tau],\,-\,y[\tau:\tau+w])$.
Only positions with $\mathcal{A}(\tau) > 0.35$ are advanced to the MDL stage.
A turbulent spike with random phase structure scores near zero on
$\mathcal{A}$ regardless of its amplitude; a genuine sign-flip scores near 1.0.
This pre-filter rejects the structural class of events that gradient detectors
accept: events with large gradient magnitude but without antipodal coherence.

\textbf{MDL gain threshold denominated in bits.}
The MDL acceptance criterion requires a compression gain of $G = 20$ bits.
This threshold is not derived from signal statistics --- it is a fixed
information-theoretic cost, calibrated so that the gain from specifying a seam
location must exceed the cost of encoding it.
As turbulence increases, both the single- and two-segment model residuals grow,
but the systematic RSS reduction from a true structural break grows too;
for noise-only fluctuations, no systematic reduction exists regardless of
amplitude (Section~\ref{subsec:stability}).
The empirical consequence, validated in Figure~\ref{fig:snr_sweep}, is that
MASS/SMASH precision remains near 1.000 from SNR 20:1 down to SNR 2:1 on this
benchmark --- but this is an \emph{empirical} finding at fixed threshold, not a
general theorem; different signal geometries or lower thresholds will produce
different behavior.

\subsection{Solar Wind as a Validation Domain}

We demonstrate MASS/SMASH on solar wind current sheet detection, a well-studied
instance of the sign-flip detection problem.
The domain is well-suited as a benchmark for several reasons: the sign-flip
signal structure is well-characterized, a high-performance comparison method
(PVI) exists, and synthetic ground truth is precisely recoverable.
The solar wind case study does not exhaust the scope of the method; we discuss
extensions in Section~\ref{sec:applications}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Change-Point Detection}

Classical change-point detection is well-developed for mean-shift, variance-shift,
and distributional-shift events \citep{Basseville1993,Truong2020}.
CUSUM \citep{Page1954} detects persistent mean offsets under sequential testing;
it is directional and requires two opposing statistics to detect sign reversals.
Binary segmentation \citep{Vostrikova1981} recursively splits the series at the
single most significant change-point.
PELT \citep{Killick2012} solves the penalized segmentation problem
$\min_m \sum_{i=1}^{m+1} C(y_{t_{i-1}+1:t_i}) + \beta m$ in $\mathcal{O}(n)$
time using dynamic programming; its cost function targets mean/variance fit
quality, not antisymmetric structure.
Bayesian online change-point detection \citep{AdamsMacKay2007} maintains a
posterior over run-length, providing principled uncertainty quantification.

None of these methods addresses the precision collapse problem identified in
Section~\ref{sec:intro}: they all use thresholds derived from signal statistics
(likelihood ratios, posterior probabilities relative to a prior, or penalization
coefficients), which are susceptible to being overwhelmed by heavy-tailed noise.
MDL-based segmentation \citep{Rissanen1978,Davis2006} comes closest to the
absolute-threshold approach: the $\log_2 N$-bit location penalty is independent
of the signal amplitude.
MASS/SMASH extends MDL segmentation with an antisymmetry pre-filter specifically
targeting sign-flip events.

\subsection{Sign-Flip and Antipodal Structure}

We are not aware of prior change-point work that uses cross-window
antisymmetry (antipodal correlation) as the primary candidate generator.
Boundary detection in image processing uses mirrored filter responses
\citep{Canny1986}, but the filter is applied to spatial edges, not temporal
sign-flip transitions.
The related concept of antipodal symmetry in algebraic topology and signal
processing has not, to our knowledge, been applied to sequential change-point
detection.

\subsection{Solar Wind Current Sheet Detection}

Automated current sheet detection in heliophysics uses primarily:
(1) the Partial Variance of Increments \citep[PVI;][]{Greco2008}, which
thresholds the normalized lagged increment $|\dBvec(t,\tau)| /
\sqrt{\langle|\dBvec|^2\rangle_T}$;
(2) directional discontinuity analysis using minimum variance or hodogram
criteria \citep{Neugebauer1984};
(3) wavelet decomposition for intermittency characterization \citep{Bruno2013}.
PVI has partial precision stability because its local variance normalization
partially compensates for turbulence; we quantify this in
Section~\ref{subsec:snr}.

\section{The MASS/SMASH Algorithm}
\label{sec:algorithm}
%% ============================================================

MASS/SMASH processes a univariate time series $y_1, \ldots, y_N$ and returns
a set of seam locations $\{\tau_1, \ldots, \tau_m\}$ with associated MDL
gain scores.
The pipeline has four stages: (1) candidate proposal from two detectors,
(2) non-maximum suppression (NMS), (3) per-configuration MDL scoring, and
(4) global MDL selection.
All components are domain-agnostic.

\subsection{Antipodal Correlation Detector}
\label{subsec:antipodal}

The antipodal correlation detector scores each candidate position $\tau$ by:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{A}(\tau) \;=\; \mathrm{corr}\!\left(y[\tau-w:\tau],\;
    -\,y[\tau:\tau+w]\right),
  \label{eq:antipodal}
\end{equation}
where $w$ is the half-window size (default 20 samples) and both windows are
$z$-score normalized before computing the Pearson correlation.
The score $\mathcal{A}(\tau) \approx 1$ when the $w$ samples immediately before
$\tau$ are temporally coherent with the negated $w$ samples after $\tau$:
the defining signature of a sign-flip event.
For turbulent fluctuations with random phase structure, both pre- and
post-window segments are uncorrelated, and $\mathcal{A}(\tau) \approx 0$.
The pre-filter is therefore effective not because of SNR (it cannot recover weak
events), but because it selects for structure, not amplitude.

Equation~\eqref{eq:antipodal} uses the forward-ordered pre-window correlated with
the forward-ordered negated post-window --- not true mirror antisymmetry, which
would require one window to be reversed.
For a narrow sign-flip transition (transition width $\ll w$), both formulas
agree; for wider transitions they differ.
We characterize the resulting localization behavior empirically in
Section~\ref{subsec:offset}.
Candidates are local maxima of $\mathcal{A}$ above threshold 0.35, retained
after NMS with minimum separation 60 s.

\subsection{Roughness Discontinuity Detector}
\label{subsec:roughness}

The roughness at position $i$ is $\mathcal{R}(i) = \sigma(\Delta y[i:i+w_r])$,
where $\Delta y$ denotes first differences and $w_r$ is the roughness window
(default 20 samples).
The detector score is the normalized roughness change:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{D}(\tau) \;=\;
    \frac{|\mathcal{R}(\tau) - \mathcal{R}(\tau-1)|}{\sigma(\mathcal{R})}.
  \label{eq:roughness}
\end{equation}
Candidates are local maxima of $\mathcal{D}$ above threshold 0.20, with NMS.
The roughness detector is complementary to the antipodal scanner: it fires
where local variance changes, which includes the edges of a wide or
turbulence-embedded transition layer.

\subsection{MDL Model Selection}
\label{subsec:mdl}

After merging the top-$k^*$ candidates from both detectors (default $k^*=3$),
MASS/SMASH scores all seam configurations with the MDL objective:
\begin{equation}
  \MDL(m, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) \;=\;
    \underbrace{\frac{N}{2}\log_2\frac{\RSS}{N}}_{\text{data fit}}
    \;+\;
    \underbrace{\frac{p}{2}\log_2 N}_{\text{model complexity}}
    \;+\;
    \underbrace{\frac{\alpha}{2}\,m\,\log_2 N}_{\text{seam encoding}},
  \label{eq:mdl}
\end{equation}
where $\RSS$ is the pooled residual sum of squares across segments, $p$ is the
total model parameter count, $m$ is the number of seams, and $\alpha = 2.0$.
The seam encoding term reflects the $\approx m\log_2 N$-bit cost of specifying
seam locations in the description.
A no-seam configuration ($m=0$) is always included, so a detection requires an
explicit positive MDL gain over the single-segment model.

Within each segment, MASS/SMASH evaluates a model zoo: Fourier series at
$K = 4, 8, 12$ harmonics, polynomials of degree 2, 3, and 5, and
autoregressive models of orders 5, 10, and 15.
The best model per segment is selected by its BIC contribution.

\subsection{Mechanism for Empirical Precision Stability}
\label{subsec:stability}

The MDL gain from introducing one seam is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\MDL \;=\; \MDL_0 - \MDL_1 \;\approx\;
    \frac{N}{2}\log_2\frac{\RSS_0}{\RSS_1} - \frac{\alpha}{2}\log_2 N,
  \label{eq:gain}
\end{equation}
where $\RSS_0$ and $\RSS_1$ are the single- and two-segment residual sums of
squares and the seam-encoding cost is $\frac{\alpha}{2}\log_2 N$ bits.

For a window of $N$ samples containing a clean sign-flip transition from
$+A$ to $-A$ at the midpoint, embedded in i.i.d.\ Gaussian noise $\sigma_n$:
the single-segment model fits the mean at zero, accumulating residual
$\RSS_0 \approx N(A^2 + \sigma_n^2)$; the two-segment model fits each half
at its mean, leaving only noise residual $\RSS_1 \approx N\sigma_n^2$.
The ratio is:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\RSS_0}{\RSS_1} \;\approx\; 1 + \left(\frac{A}{\sigma_n}\right)^2,
  \label{eq:rss_ratio}
\end{equation}
where $A/\sigma_n$ is the half-amplitude-to-noise ratio.
The MDL gain is then $(N/2)\log_2(1 + \mathrm{SNR}^2) - (\alpha/2)\log_2 N$,
which grows with both window size and SNR.
For a strong crossing (large $A/\sigma_n$), this gain far exceeds the 20-bit
threshold even at high turbulence, provided the crossing has enough polarity
contrast; the 9 weak crossings that MASS/SMASH never detects in the SNR sweep
are those where $A/\sigma_n$ is too small for the gain to reach 20 bits.

For a turbulent fluctuation with no underlying structural break,
the best-found split does not reduce the residual systematically:
$\RSS_0/\RSS_1 \approx 1$ in expectation, and
$\Delta\MDL \approx -(\alpha/2)\log_2 N < 0 < G$.
The seam-encoding penalty $(\alpha/2)\log_2 N$ is calibrated to cancel the
expected gain from the best of $N$ random splits of noise
\citep{Rissanen1978,Davis2006}, so noise-driven candidates are suppressed
regardless of their amplitude.
This is the mechanism underlying the empirically observed precision stability
in Figure~\ref{fig:snr_sweep}; it holds when the structural break provides
systematic RSS reduction, which turbulent spikes do not.
We present this as a qualitative mechanism and empirical observation rather
than a theorem: practical factors including windowing artifacts, model-zoo
selection, and cumulative-gain aggregation affect the actual gain values.

\subsection{Chunked Inference on Long Time Series}
\label{subsec:chunked}

For long time series, MASS/SMASH applies in a sliding-window fashion: windows
of length $W$ samples at step $S < W$, with $m_\mathrm{max}=1$ seam per window.
Each sample accumulates a cumulative MDL gain across all windows in which it
was selected; a global NMS pass retains peaks above threshold $G$.
For the solar wind case study: $W = 200$ samples (600 s), $S = 100$ samples
(300 s), 8{,}638 overlapping chunks for the 30-day signal;
$G = 20$ bits for the main benchmark.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{MASS/SMASH chunked inference}
\label{alg:chunked}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Signal $y[0:N]$, window $W$, step $S$, gain threshold $G$
\State Initialize $\mathrm{gain}[0:N] \gets 0$
\For{$\mathrm{start} \in \{0, S, 2S, \ldots, N-W\}$}
  \State Run MASS/SMASH on $y[\mathrm{start}:\mathrm{start}+W]$ with $m_\mathrm{max}=1$
  \If{best solution has $m>0$ and MDL gain $g>0$}
    \For{each local seam index $\tau_\ell$}
      \State $\mathrm{gain}[\mathrm{start}+\tau_\ell] \mathrel{+}= g$
    \EndFor
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State Retain positions with $\mathrm{gain} > G$; NMS with $\Delta\tau_\mathrm{min}$
\State \Return detected positions and scores
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Case Study Methodology: Solar Wind Current Sheets}
\label{sec:casestudy}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Synthetic Solar Wind Data}
\label{subsec:synth}

We validate MASS/SMASH on synthetic magnetic field time series calibrated to
mimic Wind/MFI observations during the March 2001 interval studied by
\citet{Osman2014}.
The background field is
$\hat{\mathbf{b}}_0 = (0.201, 0.201, 0.958)$ (normalized), giving
$B_0 = 6\nT$ with $\approx 96\%$ of the reversal amplitude in the $B_z$
component.
Polarity reversals are modeled as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{pol}(t) \;=\; \tanh\!\left(\frac{t - t_\mathrm{cross}}{\delta/3}\right),
  \label{eq:tanh}
\end{equation}
with transition widths $\delta \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[15\s, 60\s]$, consistent
with observed current sheet thicknesses at 1 AU \citep{Vasquez2007}.
The 120 or 28 (depending on the experiment) injection times follow a Poisson
process at $\sim$4/day with 1-hour minimum spacing.
We use the term ``polarity reversal'' to distinguish these from the large-scale
heliospheric current sheet ($\sim$1--2 crossings per solar rotation); the
synthetic rate is consistent with flux-tube boundary crossings observed near
1 AU \citep{Wilcox1965}.

Kolmogorov turbulence ($P(f) \propto |f|^{-5/3}$) is superimposed with
amplitude $\sigma_\mathrm{turb} = \sigma_\mathrm{frac} \times B_0$ per component.
The standard benchmark uses $\sigma_\mathrm{frac} = 0.30$, consistent with
$\delta B / B \sim 0.2$--$0.4$ at 1 AU \citep{Bruno2013}.
The SNR sweep varies $\sigma_\mathrm{frac}$ from 0.05 to 0.50.
True crossing centers are recorded at the exact tanh midpoints and serve as
ground truth; they are recovered deterministically by replaying the generator
with the same seed.

\subsection{Comparison Detectors}
\label{subsec:comparators}

\textbf{PVI.}
The Partial Variance of Increments \citep{Greco2008}:
\begin{equation}
  \PVI(t) \;=\; \frac{|\dBvec(t,\tau)|}{\sqrt{\langle |\dBvec|^2 \rangle_T}},
  \label{eq:pvi}
\end{equation}
with lag $\tau = 1$ sample (3 s), 90-s normalization window, threshold
$\PVI > 3.0$, minimum separation 300 s.

\textbf{$d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$ baseline.}
Threshold at $\mu + 2.5\sigma$ of:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{score}(t) \;=\; \left|\frac{d\,|\mathbf{B}|}{dt}\right|,
  \label{eq:baseline}
\end{equation}
with 300-s minimum separation.
Note that $d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$ measures the scalar magnitude derivative, not the
vector gradient $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$; a clean polarity reversal can rotate
$\mathbf{B}$ by $180^\circ$ while leaving $|\mathbf{B}|$ nearly constant.
The baseline threshold is set globally from signal statistics ($\mu$, $\sigma$
of the entire time series), which is the source of its sensitivity to the
turbulence level (Section~\ref{subsec:snr}).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{subsec:metrics}

A detected event at $\hat{\tau}$ is a true positive if within a tolerance of
an injected crossing center; each crossing is matched at most once.
We report precision $P$, recall $R$, F1, and false discovery fraction
$\FDF = 1 - P$.
Primary tolerance: 150 s (50 samples); strict tolerance: 30 s (10 samples).

For the contaminated benchmark we additionally report the \emph{confounder
hit rate}: the fraction of known non-antipodal events whose location is within
150 s of a detection.

\subsection{Rotation Angle Analysis}
\label{subsec:rotation_method}

For each detection at sample $p$, the rotation angle is:
\begin{align}
  \Bhat_\mathrm{before} &=
    \frac{\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p-w_a:p]}}{|\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p-w_a:p]}|},
  \quad
  \Bhat_\mathrm{after} \;=\;
    \frac{\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p:p+w_a]}}{|\langle\Bvec\rangle_{[p:p+w_a]}|},
  \label{eq:Bhat} \\
  \omega &= \arccos\!\left(\mathrm{clip}(\Bhat_\mathrm{before}
    \cdot \Bhat_\mathrm{after}, -1, 1)\right),
  \label{eq:omega}
\end{align}
with $w_a \in \{10, 30, 60, 120\}\s$.
We compute $\omega$ at the raw detection position and, for true positives,
at the matched injected crossing center.
Phase-randomized surrogates (100 realizations; minimum reportable $p < 0.01$)
provide a spectral null model.

Several findings emerge from Table~\ref{tab:snr_sweep}.

\textbf{MASS/SMASH precision is 1.000 at every turbulence level tested.}
No false detections are produced across the entire range 5\%--50\% turbulence.
The absolute MDL gain threshold ($G = 20$ bits) does not inflate with noise
amplitude, consistent with the theoretical argument in
Section~\ref{subsec:stability}.

\textbf{Baseline precision collapses monotonically above 20\% turbulence.}
At 20\% turbulence, the baseline produces 4 false detections (P = 0.875).
At 30\%, this grows to 146 false detections (P = 0.161, 174 total detections).
At 50\%, it produces 730 false detections (P = 0.037, 758 total detections).
The collapse occurs because the detection threshold $\mu + 2.5\sigma$ is
computed from the global distribution of $d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$, which develops
increasingly heavy tails as turbulence amplitude rises.

\textbf{PVI precision is stable at 0.944--0.947 across all levels.}
PVI's partial stability arises from its local variance normalization:
$\PVI(t) = |\dBvec(t,\tau)| / \sqrt{\langle|\dBvec|^2\rangle_T}$.
As turbulence increases, both the numerator and the normalization window
denominator increase, partially compensating each other.
However, the normalization does not provide complete immunity: PVI consistently
produces 1--2 false detections per experiment (FDF $\approx 5\%$).

\textbf{MASS/SMASH recall plateaus at 0.679 above 20\% turbulence.}
The 19 events detected at every turbulence level above 20\% are the ``strong''
crossings: those with sufficient polarity contrast relative to local turbulence
to earn a 20-bit MDL gain.
The 9 missed events are consistently the same ``weak'' crossings: those embedded
in high-variance intervals where the MDL gain remains below threshold at any
tested noise level.
This behavioral stability is informative: MASS/SMASH cleanly partitions the
injected population into a high-confidence set (19 events, always detected,
100\% precision) and a low-confidence set (9 events, never detected at 20-bit
threshold).

\textbf{At high turbulence, MASS/SMASH F1 dominates the baseline.}
At 30\% turbulence: MASS/SMASH F1 = 0.809 vs baseline 0.277.
At 50\%: MASS/SMASH F1 = 0.809 vs baseline 0.071.
In regimes where the noise amplitude is comparable to the signal amplitude,
MASS/SMASH is the only single-pass method in this benchmark that produces
a reliable catalog.

\subsection{Contaminated Benchmark: Rejection of Non-Antipodal Events}
\label{subsec:contaminated}

The contaminated benchmark tests detector precision when non-antipodal
confounding events are present alongside true crossings.
We inject 40 confounders into a 10-day synthetic signal (40 true crossings,
30\% turbulence) at positions well-separated ($>600\s$) from true crossings.
Two types of confounders are used:

\textbf{Type A --- Variance bursts}: Gaussian-hanning noise added to all three
$B$ components at the confounder location (amplitude 4 nT, width 60 s).
This creates a large local $d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$ spike and a large $|\dBvec|$,
but has random phase structure so $\mathcal{A}(\tau) \approx 0$.

\textbf{Type B --- $B_z$ magnitude dips}: $B_z$ is scaled down to 10\% of its
current value via a Gaussian notch ($\sigma \approx 15$ samples) and returns.
This creates two large gradient events (entry and exit of dip) but $B_z$
remains positive throughout --- no sign flip, $\mathcal{A}(\tau) \approx 0$.

contaminated signals.

\textbf{The baseline fires on 98\% of confounders (39/40)}.
This is the expected behavior for a gradient-threshold detector: any event
with a large $d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$, regardless of sign-flip structure, is accepted.
Notably, the baseline's apparent precision on true crossings \emph{improves}
from 0.370 (clean) to 0.506 (contaminated): the variance bursts inflate the
global $\sigma$ of $d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$, raising the threshold and suppressing
some of the turbulent false positives.
This statistical artifact underscores the instability of relative thresholds.

\textbf{MASS/SMASH fires on 18\% of confounders (7/40)}.
The antipodal pre-filter rejects most confounders because variance bursts
and magnitude dips do not produce $\mathcal{A}(\tau) > 0.35$.
The 7 confounder hits arise from the 20 variance bursts: a minority of bursts
accidentally produce Bz patterns that exceed the antipodal threshold
($\mathcal{A} > 0.35$) even though they are not true sign flips.
None of the 20 Bz dips triggered a MASS/SMASH detection (confounder hits from
dips = 0), because a purely positive-valued dip has zero antipodal correlation.
This demonstrates that the antipodal filter is most effective for non-random
(structured) confounders.

\textbf{PVI fires on 8\% of confounders (3/40)}.
PVI's local variance normalization provides complementary protection: the
normalization window partially suppresses isolated bursts, and the 3D vector
nature of PVI means Bz-only dips are diluted across components.
PVI's confounder rejection is better than MASS/SMASH's for variance bursts,
but the two detectors reject different confounder sub-populations, suggesting
that a combined MASS/SMASH + PVI pipeline would reject confounders more
effectively than either method alone.

\subsection{Detection Localization Offset}
\label{subsec:offset}

The fall from recall 0.483 (150-s tolerance) to 0.150 (30-s tolerance) in
the single-regime benchmark reveals that most MASS/SMASH true positives are
offset from the injected crossing center.
The displacement $\Delta\tau_i = \tau_i^\mathrm{MS} - \tau_i^\mathrm{inj}$
clusters near $\pm 10$ samples ($\pm 30\s$), with a spread of up to $\pm 50$
samples ($\pm 150\s$).

The mathematical inflection point of $\tanh((t-t_0)/(\delta/3))$ is at $t_0$
--- the polarity center itself --- so the offset does not arise from a
deterministic shift to a tanh sub-feature.
Instead it reflects: (1) finite chunk step size (300 s between chunk starts),
(2) MDL scoring that maximizes compressibility gain rather than antisymmetry
score, and (3) the forward-ordered correlation formula, which does not guarantee
detection at the exact midpoint for wide transitions.
The localization precision ($\approx \pm 30\s$) is $\approx 0.14\%$ of the
typical inter-event interval ($\sim$6 hours), which is acceptable for catalog
construction.
For applications requiring transition-center accuracy, a post-detection tanh
profile fit recovers sub-10-s localization.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{The Precision-Recall Trade-Off}
\label{subsec:tradeoff}

The SNR sweep makes explicit a precision-recall trade-off that is usually
implicit: methods that achieve high recall by firing on all large gradients
do so at the cost of precision collapse in high-noise regimes.
MASS/SMASH accepts a recall of $\approx 48$--68\% (depending on signal
realization) in exchange for precision stability across the full turbulence range.

Which side of this trade-off is preferable depends on the application.
For discovery surveys where cataloging every possible event is the priority,
the baseline (at low turbulence) or PVI provide better recall.
For follow-up studies where the catalog is used as input to expensive analysis
--- plasma heating correlations, EEG source localization, fault classification
--- a 100\% precision catalog of fewer events is more useful than a 4\% precision
catalog of many events.

\subsection{Precision Stability: Not Just Turbulence Invariance}
\label{subsec:stabdiscuss}

A subtlety in the SNR sweep results is that MASS/SMASH recall does not degrade
continuously with turbulence: it plateaus at 0.679 from 20\%--50\% turbulence
(always finding the same 19 events, always missing the same 9).
This is not precision-recall trading along a continuous curve; it is a
bifurcation: the 19 strong events have enough polarity contrast to earn 20-bit
MDL gains at any tested turbulence level, while the 9 weak events never do.

This behavior has a practical implication: calibrating the MDL gain threshold
on a held-out sample of labeled events would produce a stable operating point
rather than a continuous threshold-tuning exercise.
At a lower gain threshold (e.g., 5 bits), recall would increase, but the
precision-stability property would likely weaken as turbulence-driven spurious
gains become more frequent.

\subsection{Complementarity and Pipeline Design}
\label{subsec:pipeline}

The three detectors occupy distinct precision-recall operating points:
MASS/SMASH (high precision, moderate recall), PVI (very high precision, moderate
recall, better confounder rejection than MASS/SMASH), and baseline (perfect
recall, turbulence-sensitive precision).
A practical three-stage pipeline exploiting complementarity would be:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Apply PVI to obtain a high-precision ($>98\%$) initial catalog of
    strong gradient events. This is the established domain standard.
  \item Apply MASS/SMASH with a moderate gain threshold (5--10 bits) to
    supplement the PVI catalog with sign-flip events that PVI misses.
    In the single-regime benchmark, this adds 24 true events at 100\% precision
    at 30\% turbulence.
  \item Use the MDL gain score as a continuous quality ranking for downstream
    prioritization.
\end{enumerate}

The contaminated benchmark result (MASS/SMASH 18\% vs PVI 8\% confounder rate)
suggests that for datasets with many non-antipodal confounders, PVI should be
preferred as the primary detector and MASS/SMASH as the supplementary selector.

\subsection{Localization and Post-Processing}
\label{subsec:localization}

The $\pm 30\s$ localization offset is a correctable implementation artifact.
For applications where the transition center is required (reconnection timing,
seizure onset, fault location), a post-detection profile fit to the local signal
with a tanh model recovers sub-10-s accuracy.
The MDL gain score provides a natural quality indicator for prioritizing which
detections warrant this refinement.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limits}

\textbf{Synthetic data only.}
All quantitative results are from synthetic data.
Real solar wind current sheets exhibit a wider range of transition profiles,
background orientations, and co-rotating structures not represented here.
Application to Wind, ACE, Parker Solar Probe, and Solar Orbiter data is
required before performance claims extend to real observations.

\textbf{Single-component detection.}
MASS/SMASH processes only $B_z$.
The benchmark geometry ($\approx 96\%$ reversal amplitude in $B_z$) is
favorable for single-component detection.
A follow-up benchmark with randomized field orientations would test oblique
crossings; full 3-component or vector-magnitude MDL detection would improve
recall for those cases.

\textbf{Comparator strength.}
The scalar derivative $d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$ is a deliberately simple baseline.
A vector-gradient baseline $|d\mathbf{B}/dt|$ would be a stronger comparator,
particularly for clean polarity reversals where $|\mathbf{B}|$ changes little
while the field direction rotates by $\approx 180^\circ$.
The precision-collapse results in Table~\ref{tab:snr_sweep} reflect the scalar
derivative; a vector-gradient baseline would have a lower false alarm rate and
represent a more demanding test of MASS/SMASH's precision advantage.

\textbf{Single seed; limited SNR-sweep sample size.}
Each SNR sweep level uses 28 injected crossings from one 7-day realization
(seed = 42).
Recall uncertainty is $\approx \pm 0.09$ per level; the precision-stability
result (P = 1.000 throughout) rests on zero observed false positives across
7 levels --- a necessary but not sufficient condition for general stability.
Replication across multiple seeds, longer signals, and varying event rates is
needed before the precision-stability claim can be quantified with confidence
intervals.
We present this as a benchmark finding, not a certified algorithm property.

\textbf{Parameter calibration.}
The MDL gain threshold (20 bits), seam penalty ($\alpha = 2.0$), antipodal
threshold (0.35), and roughness threshold (0.20) are heuristically set.
Systematic calibration against labeled training data would provide
better-characterized operating points.

%% ============================================================
\section{Broader Applicability}
\label{sec:applications}
%% ============================================================

The precision-stability property of MASS/SMASH is most valuable in application
domains that share two characteristics: (1) the events of interest are
structural sign flips embedded in broadband noise, and (2) false detections
are costly relative to missed detections.
Four domains outside solar wind physics satisfy these criteria.

\subsection{Magnetospheric Current Sheets}

Substorm-driven current sheet flapping in the magnetospheric tail produces
$B_z$ polarity reversals at MMS and Cluster spacecraft \citep{Chasapis2015,Retino2007}.
The signal structure is identical to solar wind current sheets, but at kinetic
spatial scales and with steeper turbulence spectra.
The same algorithm applies directly with window and step sizes recalibrated to
shorter correlation times ($\sim$seconds rather than minutes).

\subsection{EEG and Neural Signals}

Reference electrode inversions produce clean sign flips in bipolar EEG channel
time series.
Automated detection currently relies on heuristic amplitude thresholds that
produce many false positives in the high-amplitude pre-ictal interval preceding
a seizure \citep{Shoeb2004} --- exactly the precision collapse regime identified
in Section~\ref{subsec:snr}.
The antipodal pre-filter would select true inversions (structured sign flips)
over pre-ictal noise bursts (unstructured), potentially improving automated
source localization pipelines.

\subsection{Climate Regime Shifts}

ENSO polarity transitions (El Niño $\to$ La Niña and back) are sign flips in
the Niño-3.4 SST anomaly index embedded in interannual variability
\citep{Trenberth1990}.
With $N \sim 10^2$--$10^3$ monthly values, the chunked-inference procedure
requires smaller windows ($\sim$24 months) and recalibration of the MDL gain
threshold.
The main application would be detecting robust phase transitions rather than
noise-driven excursions, precisely the precision-stability use case.

\subsection{AC Power Systems Fault Detection}

Line-to-line faults and inverter failure modes produce sign-flip patterns in
phase current waveforms at line frequency.
Gradient-based relay algorithms suffer from false trips during high-harmonic
distortion events --- non-antipodal signals that create large $di/dt$
but no polarity reversal.
The antipodal pre-filter would improve relay selectivity (fewer false trips)
at the cost of slightly slower trip time for the weakest faults.
Real-time implementation would require a hardware-accelerated or FPGA version
of the correlation inner loop.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%% ============================================================

We have introduced MASS/SMASH, an MDL-regularized algorithm for detecting
sign-flip change-points --- polarity reversals from $+\mu_0$ to $-\mu_0$ ---
in noisy univariate time series.
The algorithm combines an antipodal correlation pre-filter, a roughness
discontinuity detector, and an absolute MDL gain acceptance criterion.
The key contribution is a precision-stability property: unlike threshold
detectors whose false discovery fraction grows with turbulence, MASS/SMASH
precision remains invariant across the SNR range tested because the MDL gain
criterion is denominated in bits, not in signal-derived statistics.

Three solar wind case-study experiments yield the following principal findings.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Precision 1.000 across the full turbulence sweep (5\%--50\%
$\times B_0$, SNR 20:1 to 2:1).}
No false detections are produced at any tested turbulence level.
The $d|\mathbf{B}|/dt$ threshold baseline, by contrast, falls to precision
0.037 at 50\% turbulence (758 spurious detections for 28 true events).
PVI precision is stable at 0.944--0.947 across all levels due to its
local variance normalization.

\item \textbf{Confounder rejection: 18\% fire rate for MASS/SMASH vs 98\%
for the threshold baseline.}
When 40 non-antipodal confounding events (variance bursts and $B_z$ dips)
are injected alongside 40 true crossings, the baseline fires on 98\% of
confounders, MASS/SMASH on 18\%, and PVI on 8\%.
MASS/SMASH and PVI have complementary rejection mechanisms and together would
suppress nearly all tested confounder types.

\item \textbf{MASS/SMASH recall partitions the event population into two stable
subsets.}
At 30\% turbulence and above, the same 19 strong crossings are always detected
(high MDL gain) and the same 9 weak crossings are never detected (insufficient
MDL gain).
This stable partition is more useful for follow-up studies than a threshold
whose detection set changes unpredictably with noise.

\item \textbf{Complementarity with PVI: joint catalog covers 95\% of events
at $\sim$5\% combined FDF.}
Of the 58 single-regime benchmark true positives, 24 correspond to crossings
PVI missed.
A PVI-first, MASS/SMASH-supplement pipeline covers the broadest population
with manageable false discovery fraction.

\item \textbf{Rotation angle analysis: MASS/SMASH true positives are
statistically indistinguishable from PVI events when corrected for the
detection localization offset.}
The $\approx\pm 30\s$ offset is an implementation artifact of finite chunk
discretization, not a physically meaningful sub-feature.

\end{enumerate}

Future work will address: (a) validation on real spacecraft data (Wind/MFI,
Parker Solar Probe FIELDS, Solar Orbiter MAG); (b) multi-component and
vector-magnitude detection; (c) systematic MDL gain threshold calibration;
(d) real-time streaming implementation; and (e) application to EEG, climate,
and power systems domains with appropriate parameter recalibration.
